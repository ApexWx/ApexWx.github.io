7-Day Outlook: Sun. Apr. 12 – Sat. Apr. 18
Updated April 12 at 8:40 AM EDT

Today – Tuesday
A deep upper-level ridge of high pressure will dominate the US East Coast this week, with the Saint John Valley on the northern periphery of circulation around this ridge. Disturbances moving through the flow will bring chances of rain to the region over the course of the upcoming week. A warm front approaches tonight and lifts across the region Monday followed by a cold front Monday night into Tuesday.

Today, high pressure moving offshore of the Middle Atlantic brings sunny skies and continued cool conditions with a high in the mid-40s and northwest wind 5-6 mph. Tonight, skies become mostly cloudy after midnight with a 30% chance of rain this evening increasing to a 90% chance of rain overnight with some snow mixing in early Monday as temperatures fall to near or just below freezing. Temperature in the low 30s with near calm south wind increasing to 4-6 mph overnight.

A warm front moves through Monday with cloudy skies and a90% chance of rain with precipitation totals in the 1/10 to 1/4-inch range. South wind around 9 mph becoming southwest in the evening at 5-6 mph. Monday night, a cold front drops across Maine with a slight chance of showers in the evening then becoming partly cloudy behind the front with a low in the upper 30s and northwest wind 0-5 mph.

High pressure to the north in Québec builds into the Saint John Valley Tuesday with partly sunny skies. The surface ridge slides into Labrador Tuesday night with mostly cloudy skies overspreading the Valley as another warm front approaches from the southwest a head of the next low pressure system to affect the region. High Tuesday in the low 50s with light, variable wind over the course of the day. 30% chance of showers by the afternoon with 1/10-inch or less precipitation expected.

Tuesday night, warm front lifts towards the region with a 60% chance of showers with rainfall amounts in the 1/4 to 1/2-inch range (helping with drought conditions across northern Maine). Low in the mid-to-upper 30s with light northeast wind.

Wednesday – Saturday
Based on NWS data, a low pressure system will move into the area Wednesday night and pass north of the region through Thursday and Thursday night, followed by another, stronger low pressure system on Friday. While forecasts vary on the exact timing and intensity, there is a good chance the area will receive at least a 1/4-inch of rain during this period. Conditions are expected to improve Saturday as high pressure builds in, bringing dry weather, mostly clear skies, and temperatures that are as warm or slightly warmer than Friday.

On Wednesday, skies will be partly sunny with a chance of rain developing during the afternoon. High temperatures will reach the low 50s with light northeast winds. A chance of rain continues Wednesday night under mostly cloudy skies, with lows settling into the upper 30s. On Thursday, clouds will linger and rain becomes more likely, especially later in the day, with highs in the mid-50s. Rain remains likely Thursday night, mainly before early morning, with lows in the low 40s.

By Friday, conditions improve slightly with a mix of sun and clouds and just a chance of lingering showers. Highs will climb into the upper 50s, followed by partly cloudy skies Friday night and lows in the mid-30s. Saturday will be mostly sunny and warmer, with highs reaching the low to mid-60s and only a slight chance of a passing shower. Clouds increase again Saturday night as rain becomes likely, with overnight lows in the low 40s.

🇺🇸 Today's US National Weather Outlook 🇺🇸

Stormy West and Active Central U.S. Weather Pattern Continues
An active weather pattern will bring heavy mountain snow and soaking rain to California today as a strong system moves inland, with the Sierra Nevada expecting up to two feet of additional snow and lower elevations receiving around an inch of rain with gusty winds. Precipitation will spread into the Great Basin and Pacific Northwest before tapering off by Tuesday morning. Farther east, strong to severe thunderstorms with heavy rain are expected across the southern Plains, Upper Midwest, and Great Lakes, with another round possible Monday night into Tuesday. Warm air will expand across much of the eastern United States, bringing unusually high temperatures, while cooler, unsettled conditions gradually spread across the West.

⛈ Severe Weather Outlook🌪️
Learn more about Severe Thunderstorm Outlooks
Isolated Severe Storm Risk Across Texas and Northern Tier
A developing low pressure system across the northern Plains will help fuel scattered thunderstorms across parts of the country today, with the greatest concern centered over central and southern Texas. In these areas, a moist and unstable air mass will support isolated to scattered strong to severe storms this afternoon and evening. Some storms could produce large hail and gusty winds, although the exact placement and coverage of storms remains uncertain. Earlier storm activity in southeastern Texas is expected to weaken by midday, but could leave behind boundaries that help trigger new development later in the day.

Farther north, isolated severe storms are also possible across the Upper Great Lakes and northern Rockies. In Minnesota and nearby areas, a narrow corridor of moisture may support thunderstorms capable of producing hail, with a very low chance of a tornado. Across the northern Rockies, gusty winds from stronger storms will be the primary concern. Overall, while severe weather will be scattered, conditions will support pockets of intense storms.

🌧️Excessive Rainfall Outlook 🌧️
Learn more about Excessive Rainfall Risk Categories
Heavy Rain and Flooding Risk Focused on Central Texas
A developing weather pattern will bring the threat of heavy rainfall and possible flooding to central Texas, where a moist and unstable air mass will support rounds of thunderstorms through the afternoon and overnight hours. Some storms may become strong, but the primary concern will be intense rainfall rates that could exceed two inches per hour in spots. While the region is in need of rain due to ongoing drought, very dry and hardened ground may not absorb water efficiently, increasing the risk of runoff and localized flooding, especially in urban areas along the Interstate 35 corridor.

Farther north into parts of Oklahoma, Kansas, and Missouri, scattered storms may also produce locally heavy rain, though faster storm movement should limit widespread flooding concerns. In northern Michigan, additional rainfall of one to two inches could lead to localized flooding issues, particularly where snowpack remains and soils are already saturated.

🔥Fire Weather Outlook🔥
Learn more about Fire Weather Outlooks

Dry, Windy Conditions Raise Fire Weather Concerns Across Multiple Regions
A strengthening storm system moving from the northern Plains into the upper Midwest will bring widespread dry and windy conditions across much of the Southwest and Great Plains today. In the Southwest and Four Corners region, gusty winds reaching 25 to 30 miles per hour combined with very low humidity levels around 10 to 15 percent will create an increased risk for fire spread, although conditions may remain just below critical thresholds in most areas. Across the central High Plains, similarly dry air and winds of 20 to 25 miles per hour will elevate fire concerns, especially in parts of Nebraska where vegetation is more receptive. Meanwhile, portions of the Mississippi and Ohio River Valleys will also see elevated fire risk due to ongoing dryness, breezy winds, and low humidity.

How accurate are forecasts? On average, for the first 72 hours the accuracy is 90 percent. For days 4-7 it is 70 percent. After seven days, it is 50 percent—just good for indicating trends. On social media and other similar sites, posts that show a forecast model run a week out are just for clicks and likes, and are not considered reliable.

The vast majority of weather apps are automated without human oversight and can miss critical, nuanced details during severe or changing weather. Do not rely on an app for the forecast. Instead, go to weather.gov for the forecast.

Radar apps visualize precipitation intensity using color-coded echoes. However, updates are not instant, and even the most advanced composite radar mosaics can be delayed by 5 to 20 minutes by the time they reach your app.That delay means the weather you are viewing might already be miles away — or more intense than the image suggests.
