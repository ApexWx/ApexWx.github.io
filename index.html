Wed. Dec. 31 - Fri. Jan. 2
Cold, wintry conditions will continue across Maine through New Year‚Äôs Day and into the end of the week. Today will stay cold and breezy as Arctic air remains locked in place. Light snow showers and flurries will linger across northern Maine and the Central Highlands, with minor additional accumulation mainly in the North Woods and upslope areas. Gusty winds will ease compared to yesterday, but patchy blowing and drifting snow is still possible, especially in open areas where powdery snow can be easily lifted.

Clouds will increase tonight as the next storm system approaches. Snow will begin late tonight and become more widespread on New Year‚Äôs Day. The greatest travel impacts are expected across Downeast Maine, where accumulating snow is likely throughout New Year's Day, especially along the coast. Snow could also spread farther north Thursday evening, including parts of Aroostook County, depending on the exact track of the storm. Some areas may experience reduced visibility due to falling and blowing snow, and strong wind gusts could cause isolated power outages.

Behind the storm, very cold air returns. Thursday night through Friday night will bring bitter wind chills, especially across northern Maine, with dangerous cold lingering into the weekend.

Sat. Jan. 3 - Tue. Jan. 6
Cold weather will remain the main story through the weekend as a large area of low pressure stays over central Canada. This setup continues to funnel cold air into Maine with little relief. Daytime temperatures will struggle to warm, with highs mostly in the single digits to low 10s across northern Maine. Nights will be especially cold, with lows dropping to around 10 below zero. When combined with light winds, wind chills could fall into the 20s below zero across the Valley late Saturday night and again Sunday night.

Looking ahead to early and mid-week, there is growing attention on a potential storm system that could affect the region. Forecast models disagree on the exact track and strength of this system. Some guidance suggests a low pressure system could move over Maine and bring accumulating snow, while other models keep the system farther north with less impact. At this point, confidence is low on timing and amounts, but there is at least a chance for precipitation early to mid-week, and forecast details will become clearer as the system develops.

Short-Term National Outlook:  Wed. Dec. 31 - Fri. Jan. 2

Arctic Cold, Heavy Snow, and Flooding Concerns as New Year Begins
A blast of Arctic air is spreading across much of the eastern United States as the new year begins, bringing dangerous cold and significant winter weather. Heavy lake-effect snow will continue near the Great Lakes, especially downwind of Lakes Erie and Ontario, where several more feet of snow are possible. A fast-moving storm will also trigger snow showers and brief snow squalls from the Ohio Valley into Pennsylvania and western New York. Snow will spread across much of New England on New Year‚Äôs Day, with the heaviest totals likely in Downeast Maine. Gusty winds will make it feel even colder. Meanwhile, unusually warm Pacific moisture will bring heavy rain to southern California, raising concerns for flash flooding, while mountain areas farther inland see accumulating snow.

Extended National Outlook: Sat. Jan. 3 - Tue. Jan. 6

Active West Coast Storms and Southeast Rain as Cold Holds in the Northeast
A busy weather pattern will affect much of the country heading into the weekend. Cooler, unsettled conditions will remain across the Great Lakes and Northeast as a large area of low pressure lingers over eastern Canada. Farther west, a strengthening storm system will bring widespread rain and mountain snow to the West Coast, especially northern California, where heavy rain and Sierra snow are likely through Saturday. Across the Southeast, a developing low pressure system will move along a stalled front, producing a swath of moderate rain and a few thunderstorms before moving offshore Saturday. Another fast-moving system may bring light snow from the Upper Midwest through the Great Lakes and into New England late Sunday into Monday. Temperatures will be much warmer than normal from the Rockies into the Plains, while the Northeast stays colder than average, with the chilliest air lingering over Maine.

Wed. 12/31: No severe thunderstorms are expected with this system. Late tonight into early Thursday morning, a weak storm system approaching from the Pacific may trigger a few isolated thunderstorms along the southern California coast. These storms are most likely to develop near and just ahead of a weak front moving inland after midnight. Any thunderstorms that form should remain elevated, meaning they develop higher in the atmosphere rather than near the ground. Because the atmosphere will have limited energy and weak winds aloft, storms are expected to stay brief and non-severe. The main impacts would be locally heavier rain, lightning, and brief gusty winds near the coast, with activity tapering off toward Thursday morning.
Thu. 1/1: A marginal risk for severe thunderstorms has been issued by the NWS for coastal southern California on New Year‚Äôs Day morning. A compact storm system moving inland may trigger isolated to scattered thunderstorms, mainly near the coast. While storms are expected to be limited in coverage, a few could become briefly strong. The main concerns are locally damaging wind gusts and a very slight chance of a brief tornado, especially near the Los Angeles Basin. Storms will form early in the day as the system passes through. Farther inland and east into Arizona, storms are less likely and should remain weaker, with only spotty thunderstorm activity expected.
Fri. 1/2: No severe thunderstorms are expected, but some general thunderstorms may develop late Friday night into early Saturday. Across the interior Deep South and the central Gulf Coast, increasing moisture ahead of a weak disturbance could lead to isolated thunderstorms, mainly overnight. Storms should be limited in strength due to low atmospheric energy. Farther south near the Gulf Coast, a few stronger storms are possible late in the period, but confidence remains low. Along the Pacific Coast of northern California into southwest Oregon, a strong storm system will bring gusty winds and rain, with only a very small chance of isolated thunderstorms early Saturday morning. Overall, storms are expected to be scattered, brief, and non-severe.

Sat. 1/3 - Wed. 1/7: The main area to watch for stronger storms will be along the northeast Gulf Coast on Saturday, from morning into the afternoon. A fast-moving weather disturbance will cross the Southeast, allowing limited instability and moisture to spread east from the Gulf. While overall storm energy will be modest, conditions may support a marginal risk for a few stronger thunderstorms near the coast, mainly producing gusty winds. Meanwhile, along coastal California, there is some indication that brief, low-topped thunderstorms could develop this weekend as a large Pacific storm approaches. Details remain uncertain, and the risk will depend on the timing and strength of smaller weather disturbances. Beyond that, storm potential early next week appears low and uncertain.
