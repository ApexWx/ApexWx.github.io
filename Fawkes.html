7-Day Outlook: Sun. July 20 - Sat. July 26
Updated: Sun. July 20 at 9:20 am EDT

Sunday -  Tuesday
A cold front will move through Pennsylvania today bringing partly sunny skies and a chance of mainly afternoon showers and scattered/isolated thunderstorms with some isolated strong/severe storms possible with severe wind gusts being the main threat from any stronger storms that develop.

Precipitation ends this evening with Canadian high pressure building down from the north with clearing skies. Mostly sunny/clear skies are expected Monday and Tuesday with cooler daytime highs, dry conditions, and lower dew points (= less muggy).

Highs today reach the upper 80s with lows tonight in the mid-60s. Monday reaches the low 80s with overnight lows in the upper 50s. Tuesday tops out around 80 with temperatures in the lower 60s Tuesday night.


Wednesday - Saturday
High pressure shifts east/offshore midweek with southerly flow behind the high bringing warmer temperatures and higher dew points back to the Lower Susquehanna Valley region. Mostly sunny skies remain across Lancaster and Fawkes House through Friday.

A cold front looks to move through Friday with afternoon showers/thunderstorms for Lancaster and vicinity. A sight chance of showers/thunderstorms lingers Friday night into Saturday. The front may stall out near the southern border or just south of the region with partly sunny skies and another round of afternoon showers/thunderstorms for Saturday into Saturday night.

US National Weather for July 20

Severe Storms, Flash Flooding, and Dangerous Heat Dominate U.S. Weather Pattern
A complex weather pattern is unfolding across the Continental U.S., driven by a ridge of high pressure over the Southeast and an active jet stream across the northern tier. This setup is funneling deep moisture from the Gulf of Mexico into the Northern Plains, Midwest, and Ohio Valley, where it intersects a frontal boundary, sparking widespread thunderstorms. The Weather Prediction Center (WPC) has issued a Moderate Risk (level 3/4) for flash flooding today in northeast Missouri and central Illinois, with a broader Slight Risk (2/4) extending from the eastern Dakotas to the central Appalachians.

As a cold front advances through the Mid-Atlantic, the flash flood threat shifts toward the Ohio and Tennessee Valleys by Monday, with more widespread showers and thunderstorms expected across the Southeast. An approaching upper-level trough will also bring increasing flash flood risks to the Northern Plains on Monday and the Upper Midwest by Tuesday, particularly in northern Minnesota, northern Wisconsin, and Michigan's Upper Peninsula.

The stormy pattern also brings a heightened risk of severe weather. The Storm Prediction Center (SPC) has issued Slight Risks (2/5) for parts of the Dakotas, Ohio Valley, and the urban Northeast corridor—including New York City, Hartford, Providence, and Boston—where storms could bring damaging winds, large hail, and isolated tornadoes. Marginal Risks (1/5) persist into Monday for portions of the Northern Plains and Southeast.

In the Southwest, daily thunderstorms may trigger localized flash flooding, especially in Arizona, New Mexico, West Texas, and Colorado, where rugged terrain and burn scars increase vulnerability.

Meanwhile, dangerous heat is intensifying under the persistent Southeast ridge. Heat indices of 105–115°F are expected across the Southern Plains, Lower-to-Middle Mississippi Valley, and Southeast through early week. Extreme Heat Warnings and Watches cover much of the Central Plains and Middle Mississippi Valley, while Heat Advisories are in place across a broad swath of the South and Southeast. The oppressive heat will expand northward into the Midwest by mid-week as the high-pressure ridge builds.
Thunderstorm/Severe Weather Outlook
Strong to severe storms appear most likely later today into this evening across parts of the Northeast, Midwest and Ohio Valley, and northern Plains.
