⚠️ Regional Hazardous Weather Outlook ⚠️

7-Day Outlook: Friday, June 12 – Thursday, June 18
Updated June 12 at 9:45 AM EDT

Fri June 12 - Sun June 14
Hot and humid conditions will continue across the Lancaster region today as southerly flow around the western side of a Bermuda high pressure system keeps warm, moist air in place over the Lower Susquehanna Valley. A heat advisory remains in effect through the evening due to heat index values reaching the low 100s, creating potentially dangerous outdoor conditions. In addition to the heat, thunderstorms are expected to develop late this afternoon and evening, with some storms capable of becoming severe. The strongest storms may produce damaging wind gusts near 60 mph or greater along with large hail exceeding one inch in diameter.

Through the rest of today, increasing clouds and continued heat will support showers and thunderstorms developing mainly during the late afternoon and evening hours. High temperatures will reach the low to mid-90s. Tonight, any lingering thunderstorms will gradually diminish during the evening, with a low risk of additional storms early in the night. Skies will become mostly clear toward morning as cooler air moves in, with lows settling into the low 60s.

On Saturday, much drier and more stable conditions are expected as high pressure builds in. Skies will be mostly sunny, and temperatures will be seasonably warm, with highs reaching the mid to upper 80s. Saturday night will remain clear and comfortable with lows in the mid 60s.

By Sunday, as a cold front approaches from the west, moisture begins to return, leading to increasing clouds and a renewed chance for showers and thunderstorms during the afternoon and evening. Highs will again reach the upper 80s, with gusty winds developing ahead of an approaching system. Showers and thunderstorms may continue into Sunday night, with lows falling back into the low 60s.

Mon June 15 – Thu June 18
A quieter stretch of weather is expected to dominate much of the upcoming period as high pressure remains in control through midweek. This will bring mainly dry and comfortable conditions to start the week, followed by a gradual increase in moisture as a new low pressure system approaches late in the week. Temperatures will generally remain seasonable, with daytime highs ranging from the upper 70s early in the week to the mid- and upper 80s by mid- to late week, while overnight lows stay in the upper 50s to mid-60s.

Monday will feature mostly sunny skies with pleasant conditions. Monday night will be partly cloudy and cool. On Tuesday, the day begins dry with sunshine, but a few showers may develop during the afternoon, followed by a slight chance of showers early Tuesday night as a weak disturbance passes through. Wednesday will be partly to mostly sunny with a continued slight chance of showers and a modest warming trend.

By Thursday, moisture increases ahead of an approaching frontal system, bringing a better chance for showers during the afternoon and evening. Thursday night continues with the possibility of showers and a few thunderstorms as the system draws closer, marking the next organized period of unsettled weather after the quieter start to the week.

Further ahead, NOAA's Climate Prediction Center 8-14 Day outlook for June 19-25 indicates near to slightly below normal temperatures and above normal precipitation for Lancaster and vicinity.

Severe Thunderstorms Continue Across the Midwest and Great Lakes Tonight: Damaging Winds, Tornadoes, and Large Hail Possible
Severe thunderstorms will continue across parts of the Midwest and Great Lakes region this evening, bringing the threat of damaging winds, large hail, and tornadoes. Some storms may produce wind gusts exceeding 75 mph, and a few tornadoes could become strong. The greatest risk extends from portions of Illinois and Indiana into southern Michigan, where warm, humid air and favorable atmospheric conditions support intense storms. Strong to locally severe thunderstorms will also persist from Oklahoma and Texas into Missouri and northern Arkansas, with hail and damaging wind gusts possible. Farther east, clusters of thunderstorms moving through the Mid-Atlantic may continue to produce scattered wind damage before weakening later tonight. Overall storm intensity should gradually decrease overnight, although isolated severe weather remains possible.

Heavy Downpours May Cause Isolated Flooding from the Southern Rockies to the Northeast and Across Parts of Florida
Scattered thunderstorms capable of producing heavy rainfall are expected today from the southern Rockies and southern Plains through the Tennessee Valley, Mid-Atlantic, and Northeast. While most storms will continue to move steadily, some areas could experience brief periods of intense rainfall that may lead to isolated flooding, especially in locations with poor drainage or where multiple storms pass over the same area. Farther south, central and southwest Florida will see another day of very humid conditions and numerous afternoon thunderstorms. Dry, Windy Conditions Increase Wildfire Danger Across the Great Basin and Central Rockies
Dry and breezy weather will create an elevated risk for wildfire spread across parts of the Great Basin, central Rockies, and nearby High Plains today. Very low humidity levels, combined with warm temperatures and gusty west winds, will allow vegetation to dry out quickly and become more susceptible to ignition. Sustained winds of 15 to 20 mph, with locally stronger gusts in higher terrain, may help any fires that develop spread more rapidly. Areas with especially dry grasses, brush, and other vegetation will face the greatest concern. Similar conditions are expected farther north across parts of the Snake River Plain, where low humidity and breezy winds will also increase fire danger.

How accurate are forecasts? On average, for the first 72 hours the accuracy is 90 percent. For days 4-7 it is 70 percent. After seven days, it is 50 percent—just good for indicating trends. On social media and other similar sites, posts that show a forecast model run a week out are just for clicks and likes, and are not considered reliable. Extended forecasts are subject to change as weather patterns evolve so should be considered guidance rather than precise predictions. Check for updated forecasts and advisories as conditions develop.

The vast majority of weather apps are automated without human oversight and can miss critical, nuanced details during severe or changing weather. Do not rely on an app for the forecast. Instead, go to weather.gov for the forecast.

Radar apps visualize precipitation intensity using color-coded echoes. However, updates are not instant, and even the most advanced composite radar mosaics can be delayed by 5 to 20 minutes by the time they reach your app.That delay means the weather you are viewing might already be miles away — or more intense than the image suggests.
