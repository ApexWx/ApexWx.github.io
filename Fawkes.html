⚠️ Regional Hazardous Weather Outlook ⚠️





7-Day Outlook: Tue. Mar. 24 – Mon. Mar. 30
Updated March 24 at 8:37 AM EDT

Today – Thursday
High pressure over western Pennsylvania will bring dry and mostly sunny conditions through midweek. Temperatures will gradually rise to near or slightly above normal by Wednesday as south to southwest winds increase. A stronger warm-up is expected Thursday, with highs 10–20°F above average for late March, approaching—but likely not breaking—daily record highs.

Today will be sunny with highs in the upper 40s and light northwest winds around 5 to 7 mph, becoming calm by the afternoon. Tonight, clouds will increase as temperatures fall into the low to mid-30s with light winds remaining calm.

Wednesday will be mostly cloudy with highs in the low to mid-50s, and winds shifting from calm to a gentle south breeze of 5 to 9 mph during the afternoon. Wednesday night remains mostly cloudy with lows in the low 40s and south winds near 7 mph.

Thursday will bring partly sunny skies with warmer temperatures reaching the upper 60s and southwest winds 6 to 11 mph, gusting as high as 22 mph. Rain is expected Thursday night, mainly after 2 a.m., with lows in the upper 40s and an 80% chance of precipitation. Accumulations are forecast between a tenth and a quarter of an inch.

Friday – Monday
A strong cold front is expected to move southeast across central Pennsylvania Friday night, bringing the best chance for rain between late evening and early morning. While heavy rain and severe storms are more likely to stay west over the Ohio Valley and Midwest, there is a slight chance that late-night storms could reach western Pennsylvania.

Conditions east of the Alleghenies should remain more stable, which greatly reduces the risk of severe weather there. Overall, most of the area can expect steady rain with limited storm impacts, though western locations may see brief, stronger activity overnight.

Friday will start with widespread rain early, and highs reaching the upper 50s. Conditions will improve by Friday night as skies become partly cloudy, with temperatures dropping into the mid-20s. Saturday will bring sunny skies and cooler highs in the mid-40s, followed by a mostly clear night with lows near the upper 20s.

Sunday offers mostly sunny skies with highs climbing into the low 50s, while Sunday night will be partly cloudy with lows in the upper 30s. Monday continues the warming trend with partly sunny skies and highs near 60°F. Monday night will remain partly cloudy with lows around 42°F, setting the stage for a mild start to the week.

🇺🇸 Today's US National Weather Outlook 🇺🇸 (click to expand/collapse)

Record Heat Builds Before Sharp Turn to Storms and Cooler Weather
A significant warmup is underway across the central United States, with record-breaking heat spreading from the Southwest into the Plains and Mississippi Valley through midweek. High temperatures will climb into the upper 80s to upper 90s in some areas, running 25 to 35 degrees above normal and challenging daily records. The heat peaks around Wednesday before a strong cold front begins pushing south. By Thursday, this front will trigger showers and thunderstorms, some of which could become strong to severe, especially from the Mid-Mississippi Valley into the Ohio Valley. Behind the front, much cooler air arrives, with highs dropping into the 40s and 50s, or even cooler in some northern areas.

Isolated Storms Possible in Florida, But Severe Weather Not Expected
A weak cold front drifting into Florida today will bring a chance for isolated thunderstorms, mainly across central and northern parts of the state. While the atmosphere will become somewhat unstable during the afternoon, helping storms develop, overall conditions are not favorable for severe weather. Winds higher in the atmosphere will remain light, which limits storm organization and intensity. As a result, any thunderstorms that do form are expected to be brief and scattered, with typical impacts like lightning and brief downpours. Activity will likely diminish later this evening as daytime heating fades and the front stalls across the region.

Limited Fire Weather Concerns Despite Dry Conditions in the West
Fire weather concerns will remain generally low across the country today. A building area of high pressure in the western United States will bring warm and dry conditions, especially across the Southwest where very low humidity continues. However, winds are expected to stay relatively light, which will limit the overall fire risk. Some pockets of concern may briefly develop in parts of northern Nevada and southeast Wyoming, where dry and occasionally breezy conditions could elevate fire danger for short periods. Even in these areas, conditions are not expected to reach critical levels. Overall, the combination of dry air and light winds will keep fire weather threats limited.

How accurate are forecasts? On average, for the first 72 hours the accuracy is 90 percent. For days 4-7 it is 70 percent. After seven days, it is 50 percent—just good for indicating trends. On social media and other similar sites, posts that show a forecast model run a week out are just for clicks and likes, and are not considered reliable.

The vast majority of weather apps are automated without human oversight and can miss critical, nuanced details during severe or changing weather. Do not rely on an app for the forecast. Instead, go to weather.gov for the forecast.

Radar apps visualize precipitation intensity using color-coded echoes. However, updates are not instant, and even the most advanced composite radar mosaics can be delayed by 5 to 20 minutes by the time they reach your app.That delay means the weather you are viewing might already be miles away — or more intense than the image suggests.
