What is UTC Time?
(click for more)
UTC (Coordinated Universal Time), also called Zulu (Z) Time, is the standard time reference used by meteorologists, weather models, satellites, aviation, and NOAA products worldwide. During Daylight Saving Time, Eastern Daylight Time (EDT) is 4 hours behind UTC. During Standard Time, Eastern Standard Time (EST) is 5 hours behind UTC. See timeanddate.com for more time zone information.

7-Day Outlook: Thu. July 23 – Wed. July 29, 2026
Updated July 23 at 10:30 AM EDT

Thu. July 23 – Sat. July 25
A Canadian high pressure system will drift from the eastern Great Lakes into New England through the weekend, bringing a stretch of dry, comfortable weather to the Lower Susquehanna Valley. Mainly sunny to mostly clear skies, low humidity, and light winds will make for excellent conditions for outdoor activities and comfortable nights with the windows open.

This afternoon will be the coolest day of the stretch, with highs in the upper 70s to around 80 degrees, about 5 to 10 degrees below average, accompanied by a light north wind around 5 to 10 mph. Clear skies and calm winds tonight will allow temperatures to fall into the upper 50s.

Friday will feature increasing clouds but remain dry, with afternoon highs in the lower 80s, still a few degrees below average. Light east winds around 5 mph will develop during the afternoon. Friday night will be partly cloudy with lows in the lower 60s and light southeast winds becoming calm.

Seasonable warmth returns on Saturday with abundant sunshine and afternoon highs in the lower to middle 80s. Light southeast winds around 5 mph will develop during the afternoon. Saturday night will be partly cloudy with lows in the lower 60s.

The pleasant weather pattern continues into Sunday with mostly sunny skies and afternoon highs in the lower to middle 80s. Sunday night will be partly cloudy and mild, with overnight lows in the middle 60s. No precipitation is expected through the weekend. Temperatures to drop into the upper 50s to lower 60s.

Sun. July 26 – Wed. July 29
The weather pattern will become increasingly unsettled from late Sunday into the middle of next week as an upper-level trough develops over the Great Lakes and extends into the Mid-Atlantic and Northeast. Several disturbances moving through this pattern will bring increasing chances for showers and thunderstorms, along with the potential for locally heavy rainfall. There is also a risk for a few strong storms, although confidence remains low regarding where the greatest threat will develop.

Monday will begin warm and humid with intervals of sunshine and highs in the middle to upper 80s. Most daylight hours should remain dry, but showers and thunderstorms will become increasingly likely Monday evening and overnight, with a 50 percent chance of rainfall. Some storms could produce heavy downpours.

Tuesday appears to be the wettest day of the period, with showers and thunderstorms likely throughout the day and into Tuesday night. Highs will reach the middle to upper 80s, with muggy conditions continuing. Locally heavy rainfall will be possible, and a few storms could become strong depending on how the weather evolves earlier in the week.

The unsettled pattern continues into Wednesday, although rain chances decrease somewhat. Expect a mix of clouds and sunshine with scattered showers and thunderstorms possible. Afternoon highs will remain in the lower to middle 80s, with any lingering showers ending Wednesday night.

Outlooks are typically updated form 7-9 AM weekdays and 8-10 AM weekends and holidays.

Tropical Storm Bertha, Heavy Rain, and Dangerous Heat Create Widespread Weather Threats
Tropical Storm Bertha is bringing heavy rain, tropical storm conditions, and a risk of isolated flash flooding and coastal flooding along parts of the Gulf Coast, especially near Louisiana and southern Texas. Heavy rainfall is also expected across the Southeast, the Carolinas, the Tennessee Valley, and parts of the southern Mid-Atlantic through Friday, increasing the potential for flash flooding. Monsoonal moisture will continue to produce showers and thunderstorms across the Southwest and Great Basin, with localized flooding possible. Severe thunderstorms may also develop across portions of the Plains and northern tier. Meanwhile, dangerous heat will persist across the Southern Tier and expand into the Great Basin and Northern Plains, with temperatures reaching the mid-90s to above 100 degrees and heat indices exceeding 110 degrees in some areas.

Persistent Heavy Rain Raises Significant Flash Flood Threat Across the Southeast
A moderate risk of excessive rainfall continues across central and northeastern North Carolina into far southeastern Virginia, where repeated rounds of heavy rain may cause numerous flash floods through tonight. Moisture from the Gulf of Mexico and Tropical Storm Bertha is feeding slow-moving thunderstorms along a stalled front, increasing flooding concerns in urban areas, including Virginia Beach, Norfolk, Chesapeake, and Portsmouth. A slight risk of flash flooding also extends across parts of the Carolinas, Georgia, Alabama, and Mississippi, where repeated storms may overwhelm drainage systems. Additional flash flood risks are expected tonight across portions of Nebraska, Kansas, and eastern Colorado. In South Texas, Tropical Storm Bertha may bring beneficial rainfall with isolated flooding, while scattered monsoon storms could produce localized flooding across parts of Colorado, Wyoming, and Utah.

Dry Thunderstorms and Gusty Winds Increase Wildfire Danger Across the Northwest
Dry and windy weather will increase the risk of new wildfires across parts of the Pacific Northwest and Northern Rockies today. Isolated thunderstorms are expected from the Columbia Basin into the Northern Rockies, but many storms may produce little rainfall while still generating lightning capable of igniting dry vegetation. Recent hot and dry conditions have left grasses and forests highly susceptible to fire, and gusty winds will make any new or existing fires spread more quickly. The greatest concern is where dry thunderstorms overlap with low humidity and strong winds. Meanwhile, parts of the Southern Plains, especially near the Oklahoma and Texas Panhandles, will also experience elevated fire weather conditions. Dry air, warm temperatures, gusty winds, and cured grasses will create favorable conditions for wildfire starts and spread.

Isolated Wildfire Risk Develops in Northern California, Oregon, and South Carolina
Elevated fire weather conditions are expected today in parts of northern California, western and central Oregon, and portions of South Carolina. In northern California and Oregon, isolated high-based thunderstorms may develop during the afternoon and evening. Although some storms could produce light rain, dry air near the ground and fast-moving storms will limit rainfall, increasing the chance that lightning could ignite new wildfires in very dry vegetation. Cloud cover and smoke should keep storm coverage limited, but any lightning strikes could create localized fire starts. In South Carolina, gusty winds, low humidity, and dry vegetation ahead of an approaching cold front will create locally elevated wildfire conditions. Residents should avoid activities that could spark fires and remain alert for changing conditions throughout the day.

How accurate are forecasts? On average, for the first 72 hours the accuracy is 90 percent. For days 4-7 it is 70 percent. After seven days, it is 50 percent—just good for indicating trends. On social media and other similar sites, posts that show a forecast model run a week out are just for clicks and likes, and are not considered reliable. Extended forecasts are subject to change as weather patterns evolve, so should be considered guidance rather than precise predictions. Check for updated forecasts and advisories as conditions develop.

The vast majority of weather apps are automated without human oversight and can miss critical, nuanced details during severe or changing weather. Do not rely on an app for the forecast. Instead, go to weather.gov for the forecast.

Radar apps visualize precipitation intensity using color-coded echoes. However, updates are not instant, and even the most advanced composite radar mosaics can be delayed by 5 to 20 minutes by the time they reach your app.That delay means the weather you are viewing might already be miles away — or more intense than the image suggests.
